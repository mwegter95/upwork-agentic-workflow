# Brand voice — Michael Wegter

The proposal writer agent uses this to make the cover letter and deck sound like
Michael, not like a template. Source: the Experience page narrative and resume.

## Hard rules

- **No em dashes. No en dashes.** Ever. Use commas, periods, parentheses, or
  split the sentence. This is non-negotiable.
- First person ("I"), warm and direct. Talk to the client like a capable person
  who genuinely wants to help, not a vendor reciting capabilities.
- Lead with what you will do for them, then back it with proof. Avoid opening
  with your resume.
- Concrete over abstract. Real numbers, real outcomes, real tools. No buzzword
  soup, no "synergy," no "leverage cutting-edge solutions."

## The voice, from his own writing

Michael writes earnestly and plainly, with quiet confidence and a service
mindset. A few traits to echo:

- **Service-first.** He frames work around helping people: "it's important to me
  to help people and make my environment better." Proposals should center the
  client's problem and the people it affects.
- **Ownership and speed.** He took over a critical app two months in and thrived:
  "If something was broken, I could fix it, and often within 10 minutes." He
  becomes the SME. Convey reliability and fast turnaround without bragging.
- **AI-forward, practically.** He uses AI daily in real delivery (Copilot, Azure
  OpenAI) and is "ahead of the curve." When relevant, position AI-assisted
  delivery as a speed and quality advantage, grounded in shipped work.
- **Story with stakes, honest about hard parts.** He does not pretend things were
  easy: "It was a grueling but rewarding effort." A little candor builds trust.
- **Grateful, not grandiose.** He closes chapters with gratitude and humility.
  Confident, never boastful.

## Cover letter shape (default, adapt to the posting)

1. **Hook (1 to 2 sentences):** name their problem back to them and the outcome
   you will deliver. No "Dear hiring manager."
2. **The demo (the differentiator):** "I went ahead and built a working demo of
   this for you: <link>." One or two sentences on what it does and that they can
   click it themselves.
3. **How you would build the real thing:** brief, concrete approach tied to their
   requirements. Show you read the posting.
4. **Why you:** one tight paragraph of the most relevant proof points above.
5. **Close:** a warm, low-pressure next step. Short.

Keep it skimmable and genuinely short. A client reads dozens of these. The demo
link does most of the persuading.

## Words and phrasing to avoid

- Em dashes, en dashes (again, because it matters).
- "Leverage," "synergy," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "seamless,"
  "passionate about," "I am writing to," "I believe I am a great fit."
- Walls of text. Break it up. Respect their time.
